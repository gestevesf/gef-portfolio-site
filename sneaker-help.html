Introduction

Project Challenge

Sneaker Help is an app created during my UX course at CareerFoundry.

It is designed to allow users to know the hyped sneakers and fashion releases, find the best stores in town, and instantly connect with fashion stylists that can help them find perfect outfits for any occasion.

The growing sneakers market is making it very hard for its customers to keep up and know all the time which sneakers are releasing and when. So instead of following numerous Instagram pages, Sneaker Help can let them know which sneakers are releasing, when, and where to buy them.

Role

As a solo designer, I was responsible for the entire design process. Which included the competitive analysis, user interviews, surveys, personas, user journey maps, card sorting, information architecture, sketches, wireframes, user flows, usability testing, A/B testing, the style guide, and prototyping.

Duration

February 2021 - September 2021

Design Process

Understand

Problem Statement

Sneakerheads and fashion enthusiasts need a way to find sneakers and clothes because they want to stand out in their offices, events, street, or to their friends.

We will know this to be true when we see how many users have signed up, how they search for stores, and how many sessions have been booked with our stylists.

Competitive Analysis

At the beginning of the project, I researched other apps offering similar functions and services to understand what already existed on the market.

I downloaded, tested, and performed a competitive analysis on two great competitors for Sneaker Help, Wishi, and SneakerJagers.

My goal was to identify the opportunities and areas for improvement in the market. For this, I did a SWOT analysis for both apps to understand which unique features my app could have and which should be avoided.

Takeaways & Opportunities

  1. It is possible to give the sneaker community a viable, competitive, and innovative web application.
  2. Including more brands and products could reach more users.
  3. A more personalized search can facilitate the user’s search.
  4. Having stores and detailed information can help users make better-informed decisions on where to buy.

Observe

Research Goals

After, it was time for me to observe what potential users had to say on this topic. For that I I delineated my main research goals on what I wanted to find more about:

  1. User behavior and the activity of buying sneakers and clothes.
  2. Collect data points to later analyse with the problem statement.
  3. How users expect the stylists to help them.
  4. Features users would like in a stylist's sneaker app
  5. Apps and websites that the users enjoy/don’t enjoy to buy sneakers and clothes.

Interviews & Surveys

I used qualitative research, and did four interviews with potential users, over Skype, to better understand their behaviors. Secondly, I’ve created a survey, answered by 34 people in 24h to collect quantitative data to prove if the data taken from the interviews was correct.

Takeaways

Every single participant shops for clothes and sneakers. This shows that this market has lots of potentials to grow.

However, female users are not as interested in buying shoes as often, even though they mainly use sneakers.

As expected, the majority of the users prefer to search and shop online since they have more competitive prices and it’s easier to compare products between stores.

This shows how important it is to focus on having directions to websites the users would probably be using more to search for new products.

Users have shared what they would want to see most in a fashion app, and mostly are organizational features, such as filters and sorting options.

This is helpful to consider the options and filters the app should have to have a better-organized system that’s easy for our users to navigate.

Users buy mostly from impulse, and the majority buy products they have already seen on websites or through influencers.

Users are not very enthusiastic about the idea of paying for an expert.

However, they gave some ideas on how they would like the experts to help them reach their goals. This is a considerable advantage on how to best approach to change their minds.

Users have frustrations with the websites they normally use. This might be an opportunity since the app and the experts will search for them. Like this, users don't need to go through these frustrations on websites.

Users use multiple websites and frequent stores that can help see what type of clothes they might be looking for.

However, this can’t generalize by only the interviews themselves, but it gives an idea of where users buy the most.

POV

User Personas

Based on the insights I gathered from the user interviews, I’ve created three user personas with three different scenarios to help me further understand the user needs.

Ideate

User Journeys & User Flows

After creating each user persona, I needed to understand how each of them might be using the app, and for that, I developed user journeys. Creating personas, helped me visualize the initial user flows, and how could they complete their tasks to reach their goal.

Card Sorting & Sitemaps

Now that I had my personas and possible user flows delined, I drafted the first sitemap based on the information I gathered from the personas and the requirements that Sneaker Help needed.

To ensure that this sitemap was understandable, I conducted an open card sorting study in OptimalSort.

By doing a Card Sorting study, I was able to get important information on how possible users would see the application. I was separating all the information and topic in each category. While the participants from this study showed and prefer to see the information more organized in fewer categories. With this information, I created a more organized sitemap for the application, where the possible users could understand better and faster how to navigate it.

Prototype

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Following the most updated sitemap, I designed the first sketches with pen and paper, always keeping in mind what the participants from the Card Sorting study showed me.

I sketched the possible screens for the three main actions a user can take in the application, like, using the Home Page to see the most recent updates on sneaker releases and news in the sneaker and fashion world. From there they could navigate through the app and search for sneakers, clothing, and search for stylists.

Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

Always keeping in mind the Mobile-First design, I used Adobe XD to design the mid-fidelity wireframes.

I tried to change the menu bar and use a burger menu instead to see how this could work. Even though there was more screen size to work with, the navigation bar could provide users with faster navigation within the app.

High-Fidelity Wireframes

I kept using Adobe XD to design these High-Fidelity wireframes.

I changed the menu back to the navigation bar. I used simple pastel colors to give the design a more modern and younger look, keeping it minimalist, easy to understand and navigate so the users don’t need too much information on how to get around the app.

There are still some features to be included in the design. In this stage, I focused more on the three main features of the app to be used in user testing as fast as possible.

Test

Usability Testing

For the usability tests, I recruited 6 participants. Five tests were conducted in person, while the other test was conducted remotely through Zoom.

All participants are representative of the target users. They are between the ages of 27 and 36, with different backgrounds, and are used to buying clothes and sneakers online.

Goals

The goal of these tests was to assess the learnability of new users interacting with the Sneaker Help app for the first time. I measured and observed if they understood the values of the app and its core features. Such, signing up, searching for a product (sneaker), searching for an expert, and booking a session with an expert.

Objectives

  1. Observe if participants can complete the Sign Up/Login process.
  2. Determine if participants find the Onboarding helpful and useful.
  3. Determine if participants can quickly understand the navigation of the app and the core features.
  4. Observe if participants can easily search for products (sneakers, clothes).
  5. Observe if participants can search for experts and book a session with one.

Feedback & Changes

After conducting the usability tests and analyzing the results, I was able to see pain points that need to be changed since most of the difficulties the participants had were from navigation and buttons placed in the wrong place.

Preference Testing

After the usability tests and making all the changes the participants have suggested, I conducted two preference tests using Usability Hub. Participants were asked to choose between 2 similar designs and state the reason for their choice.

One on the Sign Up/Login screen and the second on the first Onboarding screen. I thought it was important to place these two tests to see if the participants preferer to see less information and bigger pictures on the screens or if they prefer more empty space with explanatory text.

Test 1

The first test gave me great statistics showing that I should proceed with the redesign to achieve better results.

Test 2

In the second test, I wasn't able to achieve such concrete results. For these, I left the initial design of the Onboarding.

Final Thoughts

Learnings

This project showed me the different challenges of creating an entire product from scratch and how the original ideas and goals can change so much from the beginning to the end.

It is hard, or impossible, to evaluate success without the proper testing from potential users. So the main improvements only came after the user testings, where participants identified small but frustrating problems and pain points.

This project helped me understand the importance of user testing in the early stages of prototyping and the relevance of the users' feedback for the development of every project.

Highlighting as well the importance of working in a team and the feedback and opinion of other designers. Since I designed this project from scratch and was to focus on my thoughts and ideas, I overlooked some important changes for the usability of this application which were brought to my attention by other designers.

These were all important changes to make the app cleaner and more user-friendly. Nevertheless, the biggest change I made was to highlight and always keep in mind the accessibility guidelines for potential users that are many times forgotten.

Future

Most features are very optimized and ready for development. However, in the first round of user testings, I felt that none of the participants were happy or would book a stylist for a personalized shopping experience. And I believe that this feature can do much more, and creating a reward system, giving benefits and discounts to the users, would create more interaction from the users.

To be able to implement this. I will focus on research to understand how this can be upgraded and more intuitive, so the app will gain more interaction and have more sessions booked. I will do this by making a new round of interviews and understanding what the users want the stylists to do or have, and how can they be more helpful in finding new outfits.

I will redesign the Stylists and the User Menu screens to implement options for the users to see their “points”, “levels” and “benefits” with the new reward system. I will then proceed to another round of user testings, focused on the Stylists section, and see how this has evolved and to see if the participants’ minds have changed regarding the use of Stylists to find new outfits.
